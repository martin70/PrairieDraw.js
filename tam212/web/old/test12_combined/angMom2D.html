Angular Momentum in 2D

The angular momentum of a body describes its tendency to continue rotational motion, analogous to the way that linear momentum describes the tendency to continue translational motion. Angular momentum in 3D is a vector quantity \(\boldsymbol{H}\), while in 2D it is a scalar \(H\). The total angular momentum of several bodies is the sum of their individual angular momenta. Angular momentum changes due to the action of moments on the body, as described by Newton’s moment equation.

Note: The angular momentum of a body depends on the reference point. We write \(\boldsymbol{H}_C\) to indicate that this angular momentum is with respect to the point \(C\).

Definition of angular momentum in 2D

Point masses

The angular momentum of a point mass \(P\) about some origin \(O\) is

\[\boldsymbol{H}_O = \boldsymbol{r}_{OP} \times m \boldsymbol{v} = \boldsymbol{r}_{OP} \times \boldsymbol{p},\]

where \(\boldsymbol{r}_{OP}\) is the position vector of the point mass, \(m\) is the mass, \(\boldsymbol{v}\) is the velocity, and \(\boldsymbol{p} = m \boldsymbol{v}\) is the linear momentum.

Example: Angular momentum of a pendulum:

Click image to start or stop animation.

A pendulum is shown with a constant downwards force due to gravity. The velocity vector is always orthogonal to the pendulum rod.

When does the angular momentum about the pin joint have maximum and minimum magnitude? Is it ever zero?

The angular momentum is maximum at the bottom position when the velocity is highest. This is because the angle between \(\boldsymbol{r}\) and \(\boldsymbol{v}\) is always \(90^\circ\) and the length of \(\boldsymbol{r}\) is constant. The angular momentum is zero when the velocity is zero, at the top of the swing.

What direction is the acceleration during the motion? Is it ever orthogonal to the velocity? Is it ever zero?

The acceleration has a radial centripetal component and an angular component that is in the same direction as the velocity on the down-swing (when \(v\) is increasing), and in the opposite direction to the velocity on the up-swing (when \(v\) is decreasing).

Rigid bodies

The angular momentum of a rigid body about its center of mass \(C\) depends on its angular velocity \(\omega\) and moment of inertia matrix \(\boldsymbol{I}_C\), with

\[\label{eqn:ang_mom_3d_rigid_body} \boldsymbol{H}_C = \boldsymbol{I}_C \boldsymbol{\omega}.\]

Properties of angular momentum

Dependence on reference point

The angular momentum of a body depends on the reference point. We write \(\boldsymbol{H}_C\) to indicate that this angular momentum is with respect to the point \(C\).

Example: Angular momentum of a particle moving in a circle:

Click image to start or stop animation.

The moving mass travels in a circular pattern with constant speed.

Is the angular momentum \(H_e\) (about point \(e\)) constant or variable?

\(H_a\) is constant, because the position vector and velocity vectors are always orthogonal and always the same length.

Is the angular momentum \(H_d\) (about point \(d\)) constant or variable? Is \(H_d\) ever zero?

\(H_d\) is varying, because the position vector changes length and also changes in angle to the velocity vector. Observe that \(H_d\) is sometimes positive and sometimes negative, and is zero when the particle is moving directly towards or away from \(d\).

Is \(H_a\) bigger or smaller than \(H_e\)? Does it depend on the particle's position?

Sometimes \(H_a\) is much larger than \(H_e\), when the particle is far from \(a\) and the velocity is orthogonal to the position vector. But sometimes \(H_a\) is much smaller, such as when it is zero.

As the particle moves around the circle, about which point is the angular momentum largest?

Because the speed is constant, the angular momentum is largest about the point which is furthest away in a direction that is most orthogonal to the velocity vector.

Example: Angular momentum of a particle moving in a square:

Click image to start or stop animation.

The moving mass travels in a square pattern with variable speed.

Are \(H_a\) and \(H_b\) equal when the particle is moving horizontally or vertically, or are they not equal at all?

\(H_a\) and \(H_b\) are equal when the particle is moving horizontally, because the component of the position vectors orthogonal to the velocity is the same.

Are \(H_a\) and \(H_d\) equal when the particle is moving horizontally or vertically, or are they not equal at all?

\(H_a\) and \(H_b\) are equal when the particle is moving horizontally, because the component of the position vectors orthogonal to the velocity is the same.

When the particle is moving horizontally, about which points is the angular momentum equal? What about when it is moving vertically?

When the particle is moving horizontally, the vertical component of position is the same for points in a horizontal row, so the angular momentum is the same about \(a,b,c\) as well as \(d,e,f\) and \(g,h,i\). Similarly, for vertical motion we need equal horizontal position components, so \(H_a,H_d,H_g\) are equal, etc.

Adding angular momenta

If we have two bodies, whether connected or not, then the total angular momentum is the sum

\[\label{eqn:ang_mom_addition} \boldsymbol{H}_O^{\rm tot} = \boldsymbol{H}_O^1 + \boldsymbol{H}_O^2\]

of the angular momenta \(\boldsymbol{H}_O^1\) and \(\boldsymbol{H}_O^2\) of bodies 1 and 2.

Note: Angular momenta can only be added together if they are both about the same reference point. It makes no sense to write \(\boldsymbol{H}_O^1 + \boldsymbol{H}_C^2\), for example.
